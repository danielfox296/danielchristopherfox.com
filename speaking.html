Who's speaking

Daniel Fox

Music Behaviorist

I came to this craft-first. I'm a working music producer and multi-instrumentalist, so I know sound from the chair where it gets made. I spent years on stages and in sessions, learning before I ever read a study how a room of strangers can move the same way at the same moment. Then I went looking for why. Now I study how music shapes customer behavior, and I read what the peer-reviewed work actually says, naming the authors, the journals, and the limits, before I make a claim from a stage. The retail floor is where I prove it, so on that turf I'm a Retail Music Behaviorist. I'm based in Denver, and I'm the founder and CEO of Entuned, where this turned into a business in 2024.

My argument is simple. Music is a language and a mastered pattern, and its effect on behavior can be designed on purpose. What I bring to a stage is the part most behavioral-science talks are missing: a practitioner who has sat in the chair.

Signature keynote

The Soul Myth: Why Music Is a Language, and Why That's Not Cold

Music isn't magic. It's mastered pattern. This talk makes the case that treating sound as a language doesn't diminish it; it's what lets us understand why a room full of strangers responds the same way. The most personal and the most provocative thing I argue, from the one chair that lets me argue it: a working musician's.

Signature talk

Designing for Permission: Moving People Without Manipulating Them

Good design removes the friction between a person and the thing they already want. It doesn't push them toward something they don't. That line is the whole ethic, and it's the line between this work and manipulation. I've watched it hold on the retail floor at Entuned, where the right sound gives a shopper permission to buy, and in public space at Dizzy Charlie's summer concerts, where it gives a crowd permission to move. The same respect for what a person came in wanting, from the shelf to the public square.

The Premium a Screen Can't Play: Music as a Brand Signal

Lighting, fixtures, and product can all be photographed and matched. The music in your room is the one premium cue a phone can't carry home. This talk walks through what music does to perceived value and how a senior buyer reads it as part of the brand, the same way they read a flagship's design.

Brand & store-experience teams · merchandising leadership

The Uncopyable Aisle: Music and the Part of Retail E-Commerce Can't Ship

A website can beat your store on price, range, and speed. It can't put a customer inside a room that sounds like your brand. This talk treats stores as media and makes the case for music as the in-store experience a screen has no way to reproduce, and what that buys you in dwell, basket, and the reason someone drives to you instead of clicking.

Retail & CX leadership · omnichannel strategy

Setting the Pace: Music, Dwell, and the Honest Version of the Tempo Story

Music sets the pace of a room, and pace shapes how long a shopper stays and how a basket fills. The honest part is that the largest study to date, 140 stores in 2025, found no guaranteed tempo effect, so fit to your customer and measurement in your own rooms are where the work lives. This is the dwell-time talk for buyers who want the real picture, not a promise.

Retail & CX leadership · operations teams

Already Paid For: The Highest-Ratio Lever in the Store

You are already paying for music in your stores, so the cost of getting it right is close to nothing against what it can move in dwell and conversion. The honest play is to measure it against a control room rather than promise a percentage, because while research links music to sales, the largest study, 140 stores in 2025, found no guaranteed tempo effect. This talk shows leadership how to run that test and read the result.

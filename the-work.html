The Work

Sound is the most powerful thing in the room nobody is managing.

I came at this from an unusual direction. The craft first, the science second, the business third. Here is the throughline.

Start with the person who walked in. Not what you want to sell them, not what the store would prefer they do, not the mood you happen to be in that day. What did they actually come for, and how do they want to be met while they get it. Give them that and you stop adding friction from your own preferences. Most rooms get this backward. They start from the owner and work outward, and the customer spends the whole visit pushing against choices that were never made with them in mind.

I learned to hear it this way as a musician. I spent years building sound from the chair where it gets made before I cared what it did to a stranger walking past a speaker. That order matters. Once you have arranged a piece of music for a room full of people you have never met, you stop thinking about what you like and start thinking about what lands.

Two and a half buttons

A person standing in a store is several things at once. They have a need that brought them in. They have an identity they carry around, the kind of person they take themselves to be. They have values, the things they will and will not spend on. Touch enough of those at once and the person feels met, like the place was set up with them in mind. Miss them and the person feels handled, even if they cannot say why.

Music is one of those buttons. Sometimes only half a button, which is why I call it two and a half. The whole board belongs to the retailer. The light, the layout, the staff, the price, the smell of the place, all of it presses on those buttons together, and anchoring an identity across a whole store is the retailer's job, not mine. I understand the whole board. I have built brands and a company of my own, and watched the thing work and fail from more than one seat. But the button I own, the one I get hired for, is the music. I would rather press one button all the way down than wave at all of them.

Music is the specialty

Here is the premise stated plainly. Music has a reputation for being soul-driven, a uniquely human, almost magical thing. After thousands of hours learning its language and playing it on stages and in sessions, I landed somewhere else. Music is patterns. It is a language, structured and learnable and shared between a maker and a listener. That is not a smaller view of music. It is a more useful one. A language can be studied, taught, and aimed on purpose, and the feeling it produces in a listener is real precisely because the patterns underneath it are real. The soul people describe is the experience of mastery, not the presence of magic.

The practical consequence is simple. If sound is craft rather than mysticism, then what it does to a person in a room is something you can design, not something you leave to whatever shipped with the speakers. Entuned is where I prove that. It makes original music for a store, tuned to that store's customers, and re-tuned continuously from what the room actually does: dwell time, basket size, conversion. A store with more than one zone gets different audio in each, because the person browsing the front of the shop is not the person deciding at the back. Every pilot runs against a matched control, so the music gets judged against the room without it. That is the part most people skip. It is the part that turns a nice idea into something you can stand behind.

Why behaviorist, not psychologist

I study behavior, what people do when the sound around them changes. I am careful with the word psychologist, which carries a clinical meaning I have not earned and do not claim. Behaviorist is the honest word. I am interested in what you can see a person do, on the floor, in the room, at the register.

Designing for permission

Sitting on top of all of it is the thing I will not bend on. The work removes friction so a person can do the thing they already came to do. It does not push anyone toward something they did not want. A shopper who walked in to buy a coat and felt rushed back out the door is someone the room failed. The right music gives that person room to take the coat to the mirror, to stand there a second longer, to decide. It grants permission. It does not manufacture desire.

I hold that line because of what sits underneath it. People get to want what they want, and the moment you start steering them toward what you want instead, you have stopped respecting that. That respect is the whole difference between this work and manipulation. The same lever that lets a person settle into a decision they were already moving toward could, in the wrong hands, be turned to corner someone. I know it can. The choice to never do that is not a footnote. It is the reason I can do the work at all and still look the customer in the eye.

Two scales of the same idea

It shows up at two sizes. On a retail floor it is permission to buy. Entuned is that one, measured in rooms with real registers. In public space it is permission to move, and for that I point to Dizzy Charlie's Music and Events. It started as a jazz booking agency. I moved it into activating public space, and the clearest piece of work was a summer concert series I designed across the whole arc of a season.

The design was all about lowering the cost of joining in. Overlapping genres on the bill pulled overlapping kinds of people, so a crowd that would not normally share a field ended up sharing one and talking to each other. The dance area sat mid-field, visible and easy to walk into, far enough from the stage that the volume did not push people off, and not so central that the first person to stand up felt exposed. Free dance lessons once the crowd loosened, so nobody had to be the one who knew the steps. Vendors toward the back, where the volume was kinder and there was room to mill. What it added up to was a place that had been close to closing turning into a returning community of people who kept coming back. Same idea as the sales floor. Take away the small reasons a person holds back, and they will do the thing they half wanted to do anyway. One reads at the scale of a single shop. The other reads at the scale of a town square.

Where I am sitting when I do this

I do this as a working producer and multi-instrumentalist, not from a desk looking in. I design behavior with a musician's ear, which means I think in timing and arrangement, in when to bring an element in and when to hold it back, the way you would build a track that has to carry a room. I know sound from the chair where it gets made. That is a different thing from knowing about it, and on a sales floor or in a field at dusk, it is the thing that decides whether the room moves or just plays.

If that is your problem too, the writing and the talks are where it keeps going.

Can music make your store feel more expensive?

Yes, measurably. In the 1993 wine-store test, classical music moved shoppers to pricier bottles at the same shelf prices, and lowering the volume alone has raised checks about 15 percent. The condition is fit: music that clashes with the brand destroys value instead.

Perceived value is one of the most powerful and least used levers in retail, and music moves it directly. You probably already know which of those two wine stores wins, and so does the research.

Genre sets the quality tier

The cleanest demonstration is the 1993 wine-store experiment in Advances in Consumer Research. With classical playing instead of top 40, customers bought the same number of bottles at higher price points. Same inventory, same prices, same staff. The music changed which quality tier felt appropriate for the room, and hands followed. The spending side of that research, the restaurant replication and the two conditions that decide whether the lift shows up at all, is covered in can the right music make customers pay more? This note is about the perception side, and that runs deeper than genre.

Volume is its own signal

A subtler one most people miss. A 2003 study in Perceptual and Motor Skills tested volume in restaurants. Soft music, just lower volume rather than a specific genre, raised the average table tab by about $3, roughly 16 percent. Genre didn't matter. Volume did. Soft music signals quality. The room where conversation flows easily feels more refined than the one you have to shout across, and refined reads as worth spending in. Most stores run a little loud.

Bad fit destroys value

The other direction is real too. A 2006 study in the Journal of Business Research showed music can actively destroy perceived value. When the music clashes with the brand, customers don't just ignore it. They rate the brand as less authentic, less coherent, less trustworthy. Premium leather goods under dorm-party music make the products look cheaper. The soundtrack drags the whole perception down with it.

The actual point

Music shapes perceived value through at least three channels. Genre builds quality associations. Volume signals refinement. Brand fit either backs up what everything else in the store says or contradicts it. There's no neutral setting. If the music is an afterthought, your perceived value has a leak you haven't found.

Research note · Updated July 2026

Does store music influence shoppers without them noticing?

Yes. The same study that produced a 5-to-1 sales swing found 86 percent of shoppers denying the music had any influence. And here is the stranger finding: I coded ten landmark field experiments from 1966 to 2019, and exactly two ever asked the shoppers at all. Both found the same gap.

If I told you that playing French accordion music in a wine shop made customers buy five times more French wine than German, and that 86 percent of those customers swore the music had zero influence, would you believe me? That's what happened, and the gap between the behavior and the explanation is the whole subject.

The wine study, in detail

It ran in the Journal of Applied Psychology in 1999, striking enough that Nature ran a brief on it. A supermarket wine display, French and German wines matched on price and sweetness, stereotypically French or German music on alternating days. On French-music days, French wine outsold German 40 bottles to 8. On German-music days, German outsold French 22 to 12. Then the researchers questionnaired 44 of the buyers. Asked open-ended why they chose their wine, exactly 1 of the 44 mentioned the music. Asked point-blank whether the music had influenced their choice, 6 of 44 said yes. The other 86 percent said no while holding the bottle the music had steered them to. The music didn't suggest anything. It primed, below the level anyone could report.

I went looking for who ever asked

That questionnaire is rarer than it looks. In July 2026 I coded the landmark field experiments this field runs on, the ones every vendor deck quotes: ten studies between 1966 and 2019, in supermarkets, restaurants, bars, a department store, a wine cellar, a café. Real venues, real customers, real receipts. For each one I checked a single thing: did anyone ask the shoppers about the music afterward?

I found exactly two that did. The wine study above, and Milliman's 1982 supermarket experiment, the origin of the famous 38 percent sales swing. Milliman ran 216 exit interviews asking shoppers whether they recalled music playing while they shopped. Recall came back statistically identical whether the store had played slow music, fast music, or no music at all (the paper reports p = .97). Twenty-one of the 72 shoppers interviewed on no-music days recalled music anyway. His conclusion was careful: shoppers were "something less than totally conscious" of the music, which sat in the background of their perceptual field while the register moved 38 percent.

The other eight studies measured what customers did, sometimes what they felt, and never whether they connected any of it to the sound. Areni and Kim wanted to ask and couldn't; store management barred anything intrusive, so the classical-music effect on wine spending stands with no report from the people who did the spending. Two caveats belong here, because the authors themselves make them. North's team notes that some customers may have known perfectly well and preferred not to admit it to a stranger with a clipboard. And none of this licenses the word subliminal. The defensible claim is narrower and stranger: across four decades of field experiments, behavior moved every time, and on the two occasions anyone checked, awareness didn't.

It plugs into a bigger model

A 1982 study in the Journal of Retailing, Donovan and Rossiter, brought the PAD framework, pleasure, arousal, dominance, from Mehrabian and Russell's psychology work onto the retail floor, with pleasure the strongest predictor of whether customers approach or avoid. The same team later validated it with real receipts: the pleasure a shopper felt in the first five minutes predicted how much unplanned buying they did. Not survey intentions, actual spending. A 1997 study in Psychology and Marketing confirmed the chain, environment to emotion to behavior, and that impulse buying is an emotional response to conditions rather than a personality trait.

It even bends time

A study in the Journal of Business Research found music changes how people experience time itself. Unfamiliar music, longer stays that feel shorter. Familiar music, the reverse. The right music keeps customers in the store longer while they feel like they stayed less. More browsing, no impatience.

And it works as a throttle

One of the earliest experiments, a 1966 study in the Journal of Applied Psychology, found loud music moved people through faster without reducing total spend. Volume controls foot traffic, not purchase behavior. A study in Frontiers in Psychology found fast tempo increased variety-seeking, customers reaching for a wider range. Music is shifting pace, movement, and decision style at the same time.

The actual point

This is what makes music the most underused tool in retail. It works at a subconscious level, priming choices, shaping emotion, bending time. The customer still feels in control. Every choice still feels like theirs. The context around the choice has been arranged. Most stores leave that arrangement to whoever picked the playlist because they liked it.

This is one thread of a bigger picture. The full read on store music and customer behavior sets all of it out in order.

Common questions

Do shoppers know when store music is influencing them?

Mostly no, in the two field experiments that measured it. In the 1999 wine study, only 1 of 44 questioned buyers spontaneously named the music as a reason for their choice, and only 6 of 44 said yes when asked directly, while the music drove a five-to-one swing in what sold. In Milliman's 1982 supermarket study, exit interviews found shoppers recalled music at statistically identical rates whether slow, fast, or no music was playing.

How many store-music studies have actually asked shoppers about the music?

Daniel Fox coded ten landmark field experiments from 1966 to 2019, in supermarkets, restaurants, bars, and a wine cellar, and found exactly two that asked shoppers about the music afterward: Milliman 1982 and North 1999. Both found the same gap between measured behavior and what shoppers could report.

Does this mean store music is subliminal manipulation?

The studies don't support that word. Milliman wrote that shoppers were "something less than totally conscious" of the music rather than completely unaware, and the wine-study authors flag that some customers may simply not have wanted to admit the influence. The defensible claim is the gap: behavior moved measurably while self-reported awareness stayed near zero.

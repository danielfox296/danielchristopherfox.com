Every Room Is Already Scored

The sound in a space is changing what the people in it do. Most of the time nobody chose it for that. Soon someone will.

You have walked into a store and slowed down without deciding to. You have sat in a restaurant that pushed the volume up after nine and found yourself talking louder, eating quicker, ordering one more round you had not planned on. You have stood in a line where the sound made the wait feel longer than the clock said it was. None of that was your idea. The room did it.

Sound is the input in a physical space that moves people the most and gets chosen on purpose the least. The lighting in a building was designed by someone. The layout was drawn and argued over. The materials were specified down to the finish on the door handles. The sound is usually whatever a manager likes, or the default that shipped with the speakers, or a station nobody has touched in two years. The most behaviorally active thing in the room runs on autopilot, and most people who run rooms treat it as decoration.

I came at this as a musician first, which means I spent years learning how sound is actually built before I ever cared what it did to a stranger's behavior. That order matters. When you know music from the inside, from the chair where you make it, you stop hearing it as atmosphere and start hearing it as a set of decisions, any one of which lands on a listener whether the listener notices or not.

Where you can see the receipt

Retail is where this stops being a nice idea and turns into a number, because retail keeps a register. The famous example is a 1982 supermarket study where slow music produced meaningfully higher daily sales than fast. People repeat that one finding as if it settled the matter. It did not. When a much larger effort tried to reproduce the tempo effect across many stores, the overall result came back at roughly nothing. The honest account is the one in between. Tempo moves people, and how much depends on the key the music is in, how crowded the floor is, and who is actually standing on it.

I work on the sales floor first for one reason. It is the rare room that hands you the receipt. Dwell time, basket size, what someone is willing to pay, all of it gets logged, so you can run the music as an experiment instead of a hunch and find out what the sound did rather than what you hoped it did. The floor is not the only place the effect is real. It is the place the effect is countable.

The same thing, every other room

Take the mechanism off the sales floor and it keeps working, because it was never about commerce. It is about people. The restaurant that wants tables to turn faster has a tempo for that, and the one that wants you to settle in and order dessert has a different one. The gym has known for years that the right track pulls a few more reps out of a tired body. A waiting room can make twenty minutes feel like ten or like forty without changing the clock on the wall. The office open-plan that everyone complains about is partly a sound problem nobody framed as one. Your own kitchen on a Sunday morning is running the same machinery, and you set it yourself without calling it design.

In every one of those rooms the sound is doing real work, and in almost every one of them no one chose it for the work it does. That is the strange standing fact of the built world. We have a powerful, well-studied way to shape how people feel and move through a space, and we have left it on the default setting nearly everywhere.

The part that is about to change

For most of history the sound in a room was fixed. A record, a radio station, later a playlist. One thing for everyone who walked in, holding steady all day regardless of who was there or what the place needed in that hour. That constraint is coming apart. Generative and adaptive music is making it cheap to produce sound that responds, audio written for this room, this crowd, this time of day, and re-written as the room fills and empties. The fixed playlist is going to look like gas lamps look now.

So within ten years a lot of physical spaces will be scored the way a film is scored, tuned to what is happening in them and to what they are for, in real time. Someone is going to design that sound. The question worth sitting with is who. The people who design physical space, the architects and the operators, mostly do not yet think about sound as behavior. The people who study sound and behavior mostly do not build anything you can walk into. That gap is wide open right now, and it will not stay open.

A thing that can move a room full of people the way the right music can is not neutral, and pretending it is the background does not make it neutral. It makes it unexamined. The work I care about is naming the lever out loud and measuring what it actually does, in real rooms, with the receipts in hand. Whatever is playing where you are sitting right now was aimed at you by someone, or by no one. Both were a choice.
